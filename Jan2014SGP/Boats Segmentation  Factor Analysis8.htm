Boats Segmentation: Factor Analysis

Business Decisions

In response to a slow down in the boating market, CreeqBoat is looking into different growth opportunities, mainly in the North America region. In order to effectively assess their options and potential, CreeqBoat needs to better understand their customers and their purchasing preference and behaviors. They will need to analzye which different segments of customers are in the market, what each specific segment wants and needs in a boat and what CreeqBoat could or should offer them.

In order to arrive at such a business strategy, CreeqBoat has approached a market research firm to gather data in the US market on boating and boating preferences.

The Data

The data used as a basis for the segmentation were the results of a survey on boating behavior, preference and attitudes. Here, 29 questions were asked and scored by almost 3,000 boat owners or people intending to buy a boat. The questions were scored on a 5-point scale, making the results of the survey metric data. As 29 different attitudes and behaviors will be too complex and varied to use to determine different clusters of respondents in the data, we will analyze whether we can reduce the number of variables, whilst maintaining as much of the variance within the data as possible. With this method, we will be able to derive a set of variables or combinations of variables, called factors, to simplify our analysis of the different segments within the survey population.

Our Approach

Factor Analysis in 6 steps

  1. Confirm the data in metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Check the correlation matrix to see if Factor Analysis makes sense

  4. Develop a scree plot and decide on the number of factors to be derived

  5. Interpret the factors (consider factor rotations - technical but useful)

  6. Save factor scores for subsequent analyses

Step 2: Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

As clearly seen above, the resulting survey data is metric, as a 5-point metric scale was used to collect responses. Even though the same scales were used, the questions have different means and variance within their respective answers. In order for us to compare, link or cluster them, we must ensure they are comparable and of comparable weights. If we keep the data as is, that would mean that questions that in general get a higher score as a response will be weighed stronger than the questions with lower scores on average. However, we are looking to find different motivations for different groups of people and the difference in the absolute height of the score does not inform us of this. We should analyze the variance among answers and between questions, not the absolute score. Therefore, we should scale the data in such a way that all questions have a mean response of 0 and standard deviation of 1. This will remove the bias of weighting by absolute score, while retaining the variance between respondents in the data set.

ProjectDatafactor_scaled = apply(ProjectDataFactor, 2, function(r) {
    if (sd(r) != 0) 
        res = (r - mean(r))/sd(r) else res = 0 * r
    res
})

Step 3: Check correlation matrix to see if Factor Analysis makes sense

The complexity, or the number of variables in our data set used for the segmentation analysis, can only be reduced in case several variables are proven to contain similar information about the survey respondents. In other words, it only makes sense to remove or cluster variables if that does not significantly reduce the variance of the data. In order to ensure this it only makes sense to group variables into factors when they are correlated, or in other words, seem to explain the same attitudes and motivations of the respondents. If two variables are highly correlated, one can disregard one of them and still explain movements in the data with the other variable. Therefore, in order to know whether we can use factor analysis to reduce complexity in our analysis, we should visually inspect the correlation matrix, to see the correlation between different variables.

Step 4. Develop a scree plot and decide on the number of factors to be derived

An important question in factor analysis is how many factors are needed in order to reduce complexity whilst safe guarding that as much variance as possible is maintained. In the end, this is a judgement call, however several supporting methods are available to help us decide. The first is the so-called eigenvalue of the factors used. The eigenvalue reflects how much variance is captured by the variables that are combined within the factor. Comparing different eigenvalues tells us which factor explains more of the variance in the data than others. Below is a table in which the eigenvalues of different factors are shown. Furthermore, we can see how much of the variance is captured by different factors. This will help us decide how many factors we would like to include in our segmentation analysis and which amount of variance we would like to maintain and explain.




As a rule of thumb, factors are suggested to be included when their eigenvalue is higher than one. Here that implies we should use at least the first five factors of the different variables. However, the subsequent factors after the first five have eigenvalues close to one and it may therefore help to inspect a visual representation of the eigenvalues, called a scree plot, in order to select a sufficient number of factors.

The scree plot shows us the eigenvalues of the different factor combinations. Another rule of thumb that we can use here is that one should use the factors upto the elbow in the scree plot. When looking at the scree plot of the survey data questions, we see it has effectively two elbows. One after the first factor and another one after the sixth factor. This indicates that we may be better off including the sixth factor in our segmentation analysis as well. We also see that after the sixth variable the scree plot flattens and adding many subsequent factors does not add considerable information.

We therefore decided to iterate with 5 up to 7 factors, to analyze our data.

As mentioned above, the eigenvalues and scree plot indicated we should analyze the data using around five factors. When we performed the analysis resulting in five factors, there were a number of questions or variables that came up in several different factors. Furthermore, the first factor used seemed to contain a lot of questions, which seemed to reflect slightly different attitudes or motivations. We therefore decided to increase the number of factors to six. When using six factors, the overlap between factors using the same variables disappeared and the first large factor from the previous exercise fell apart into two seperate factors that to us seemed to better reflected a single group or motivation. When increasing the number of factors even further, the factors above six only included single variables, that to us seemed not to add significant or unique explanantions of people's motivation towards boating. That together with the low eigenvalues and their position beyond the elbow in the scree plot convinced us that we should not include any of the factors beyond six.

When using six factors, the following six motivations or attitudes among our survey respondents seem to arise:

1) Adventure and recreation. The variables that correlate significantly under this factor cover the questions number 21, 18, 19, 24, 6 and 22. It reflects a motivation to use a boat to escape from normal/daily life, to socialize with friends and family, experience nature and get a sense of adventure. The factor portrays a motivation for activity and recreation.The strongest variable within the factor is the survey statement about using a boat to escape from everyday life.

2) Passion. This factor contains variables or survey questions that reflect a passion for boating. The questions grouped here are number 27, 28, 16, 17, 20 and 23. These survey questions cover attitudes like boating being the number one thing to do in spare time, boating as a true passion, being knowledgeable and an expert about boating or always doing baoting related activities.

3) Status and power. The third factor connects variables that reflect an attitude of status or achievement which a boat may give you. Questions that are included in this factor are 9, 3, 7, 26, 13 and 14, and these questions convey a feeling of status connected to the boat or its brand, a feeling of achievement, a care for the brand and novelty of the product and its accessories as well as the power it exudes.

4)Technology and quality. More distinctly than in other factors, this factor clearly states that there is a motivation to choose boats dependent on the technological aspects or the quality of the product, either real or perceived based on the brand. The factor combines questions 4 and 5, of which question 4 about technological aspects is the strongest influencer.

5) Basic and price sensitive. The fifth factor combines questions 2, 12, 8 and 10. These questions address the topic of price sensitivity, where geeting the lowest price is more important than the brand of the boat, and getting a basic and functional design of the boat, instead of a more elaborate, stylish appearance with frills.

6) Hands on expertise. The last factor we have included contains question number 11, which reflects respondents tendency to want to be hands on and repare their boats themselves. As explained above, apparently this motivation or this sixth factor is distinct enough to cover significant variation within the data and must therefore be taken into account as a factor for subsequent analysis.

The six factors reflect that there are six distinct motivations found in the data that capture a significant amount of the variance. These six distinct demeanors towards boating that we found through factor analysis can now be used to see whether different clusters of respondents exist, based on their reaction to the survey questions and factors we have identified.

Intepretation, Decisions and Conclusion

To determine the behavior of the consumers in the boating market and develop a suitable sales strategy, the results of a consumer survey needed to be analyzed in order to identify customer segments. As a first step, we analyzed the survey questions about boating behavior, preference and attitudes. Using factor analysis we found that not all 29 variables were equally explaining different customer motivations for boat ownership and buying preference. We found that there are six distinct motivations under which many of the survey questions can be clustered, whilst still explaining a significant amount of variation within the data. Although based on eigenvalue analysis only the first five factors were indicated to be required, we included a sixth one to arrive at more exclusive, unique factors that reflect distinct consumer motivations. The folowing six factors were found:

1) Recreation and adventure 2) Passion 3) Status and power 4) Technology and quality 5) Basic and price sensitive 6) Hands on expertise

Based on these six distinst motivations, we can now perform a cluster analysis, to analyze whether there are seperate clusters of consumers who respond significantly different to other clusters on these six drivers of preference and boating behavior.
