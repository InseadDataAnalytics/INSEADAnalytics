Business Decisions

The objective of the current Factor Analysis is to simplify the multitude of criteria customers consider when purchasing boats. It allows to have a manageable list of factors that logically and objectively represent the ingredients our company needs to focus on when designing and buildings boats so that they sell well in the current market.

The Data

Our primary data is sources from a survey of 29 questions that reveal customer personalities, tastes, and intentions. The survey consists of 2,813 responses that we believe are statistically representative of our customer base.

Our Approach

Factor Analysis in 6 steps

  1. Confirm the data in metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Check the correlation matrix to see if Factor Analysis makes sense

  4. Develop a scree plot and decide on the number of factors to be derived

  5. Interpret the factors (consider factor rotations - technical but useful)

  6. Save factor scores for subsequent analyses

Step 2: Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

We have decided to scale the data to help us in the comparison of the descriptive statistics between questions. This procedure helps us reduce the relative weight in our statistical analysis of questions such as “I tend to perform minor boat repairs and maintenance on my own”, which are characterized by high mean and standard deviation.

ProjectDatafactor_scaled = apply(ProjectDataFactor, 2, function(r) {
    if (sd(r) != 0) 
        res = (r - mean(r))/sd(r) else res = 0 * r
    res
})

Step 4. Develop a scree plot and decide on the number of factors to be derived

Based on the “Cumulative percentage of variation explained” from the table below, we find that the first eight factors capture 61% of the variance. While increasing the number of factors leads to more information being captured, it does complicate the storyline and forces us to make decisions based on slim support from the data due to the low level of variability.




The scree plot visually confirms that moving to more than eight factors provides diminishing returns of information value. While factors 6 to 8 are with eigenvalue below 1, we feel that they complement well our intuition on the way customers make decisions about puchasing of boats.

We eliminate the fifth factor although it has an eigenvalue exceeding 1, because it fails to capture sufficiently differentiated information that would enrich the story on how customers make decisions when purchasing boats.

5. Interpret the factors

For our data, the 8 selected factors look as follows after the varimax rotation:



To better visualize and interpret the factors we often “supress” loadings with small values, e.g. with absolute values smaller than 0.5. In this case our factors look as follows after suppressing the small numbers:



Based on the questions with the highest loadings for each factor, we can deduce that the criteria customers look at when puchasing boats are:

1. Identity - Q1_27_Boating is the number one thing I do with my spare time
2. Experience - Q1_21_Boating helps me escape from everyday life and relax
3. Status - Q1_9_I see my boat as a status symbol
4. Economy - Q1_12_When it comes to boating I tend to prefer a basic boat with little to no frills
5. Brand - Q1_4_I only consider buying a boat from a reputable brand
6. Maintenance - Q1_11_I tend to perform minor boat repairs and maintenance on my own
7. Research - Q1_1_When buying a boat I do a lot of shopping around and visit multiple dealers
8. Functionality - Q1_8_When buying a boat functionality is more important than style

Intepretation, Decisions and Conclusion

By performing the process of dimensionality reduction, we now have more clear criteria on how customers look at boats. Our eight factos allow us to break down how we think about boats from teh customers' perspective. Furthermore, some of these factors provide us with a framework to compare out products to those of competitors.
